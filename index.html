The River That Nairobi Forgot — And Is Desperately Trying to Remember

A city's conscience flows, filthy and forgotten, through its own heart. Can a Sh50 billion gamble finally bring it back to life before another flood season claims more lives?

GM
Jeff M. FCA Correspondent, Environment & Urban Affairs  ·  June 13, 2026  ·  8 min read

Photo: The Nairobi River as it flows through Kamukunji — once a lifeline, now a dumping ground for industrial and domestic waste. Regeneration works are underway, but the scale of damage runs deep. [Nation Media / Archive, 2025]

Stand on the banks of the Nairobi River at Kamukunji on any given morning and you will be confronted by a smell that no amount of city planning language can sanitize. The water — if one is still generous enough to call it that — runs black and brackish, threaded with plastic, streaked with industrial effluent, and thick with the accumulated negligence of decades. Children play nearby. Women wash. Life, somehow, persists.

This is the Nairobi River. It is, in the same breath, a national embarrassment and a symbol of everything this city has done wrong to its most foundational natural asset.

A River Born Clean

The Nairobi River did not begin this way. It originates from the northwestern outskirts of the capital, carving a course through residential sprawl before winding southeast toward the plains. For generations before the city swallowed the landscape, it was a source of water, sustenance, and identity. The very name Nairobi derives from the Maasai phrase Enkare Nyirobi — "cool waters. " The irony today is almost too painful to contemplate.

Rapid, largely unplanned urbanisation through the 1970s, 80s, and 90s turned the river into a receptacle. Industries dumped. Settlements expanded onto riparian land. The Dandora dumpsite — one of the largest in East Africa — leached toxins directly into the watershed. The river became heavily contaminated with heavy metals and toxic substances harmful to both humans and animals. A lack of effective waste management, inadequate enforcement of environmental regulations, and insufficient public awareness compounded the damage year after year.

This is about restoring Nairobi's heart, reclaiming our rivers as public assets and proving that inclusive, climate-smart development is possible.

— Rtd Brig Joseph Muracia, Nairobi Rivers Commission CEO

The Sh50 Billion Bet

In March 2025, President William Ruto stood at Kamukunji and launched what the government calls the most ambitious environmental intervention in Kenya's post-independence history. The Nairobi River Regeneration Project seeks to restore 27.2 kilometres of river and tributaries, reclaim riparian land, cut pollution points by up to 90 per cent, and resettle vulnerable communities — without illegal evictions.

Funded by the Kenyan government and implemented by China Energy Engineering Group, the project runs alongside an affordable housing programme at a combined cost of Sh50 billion — approximately $388 million USD — over four years. The first groundbreaking ceremony was presided over by President Ruto himself, with senior government officials and environmental advocates in attendance.

Five Pillars of Restoration

The project has been divided into five core components currently being executed by China Engineering Group at sites in Kamukunji, Korogocho, Lucky Summer, and City Cotton in Buruburu. These are: flood control works; new sewer lines along the river to manage liquid waste; construction of bridges and pedestrian walkways; and the greening and restoration of catchment areas. A 50-kilometre sewer line — described by Governor Sakaja as one that will serve the city for the next fifty years — is already under construction.

Beyond infrastructure, the Dandora Dumpsite, long a source of leachate seeping into the river, is earmarked for closure and conversion into a circular economy and waste-to-energy facility — a move that could create green jobs and position Nairobi as a leader in sustainable waste management.

When the River Bites Back

The urgency of the work became brutally clear this past March. On the night of 6–7 March 2026, heavy rainfall caused the Nairobi River to burst its banks, inundating roads across the city. Vehicles were swept away. Motorists were stranded. Major transport routes — Mombasa Road, Uhuru Highway — were paralysed. Operations at Jomo Kenyatta International Airport were disrupted, forcing some flights to divert to Mombasa. The death toll from the flooding climbed above one hundred across thirty counties.

A river strangled by decades of unmanaged waste and encroachment has no capacity to absorb rainfall. The floods were not merely a weather event. They were the inevitable consequence of a city that built over its own drainage system and called it development.

The air, once clear, is now thick with the stench of waste. Respiratory illnesses — asthma and chronic bronchitis — have surged. And still the effluent flows.

— Ruai Residents, Jan. 2026 Community Report

The Problem Nobody Wants to Name

Even as the regeneration programme rolls forward, the ground reality in some areas tells a more troubling story. In Ruai, residents near the sewer plant report that unchecked industrial dumping and malfunctioning treatment infrastructure are systematically undoing clean-up efforts. Community members describe a landscape where the promise of a restored ecosystem is choked by industrial effluent and toxic methane emissions — a documented surge in respiratory illness has followed.

Governor Sakaja has acknowledged that full river restoration — across the Nairobi, Mathare, and Ngong rivers — will require an estimated Sh180 billion, at roughly Sh60 billion per river. The county cannot raise that sum alone. The national government and international partners must step in. And they must stay in.

This is the paradox at the heart of the Nairobi River story. The same city generating the political will to restore the river is still home to the industries, the enforcement gaps, and the institutional failures that destroyed it in the first place. Any relocation of riverbank communities will follow fair, lawful protocols, officials insist — but Nairobi's civil society has watched too many promises evaporate under the heat of political and commercial interests.

A River as a Mirror

The Nairobi River is not merely an environmental story. It is a story about governance — about who bears the cost of negligence and who reaps the rewards of recovery. It is a story about a city of five million people that has grown faster than its infrastructure, its institutions, and perhaps its conscience.

If the regeneration programme succeeds by 2027, Nairobi will have done something rare: repaired, at great cost, what decades of indifference destroyed. The river could once again become a public asset — walkways, green corridors, cleaner air, reduced flooding, restored dignity for communities that have lived beside an open sewer for generations.

If it stalls — strangled by politics, elite capture, or budget shortfalls — it will join the long list of promising Kenyan mega-projects that exist most vividly in press releases and groundbreaking ceremonies.

The river flows on, for now, carrying its burden of plastic and promise in equal measure. Nairobi is watching. So is the river.
